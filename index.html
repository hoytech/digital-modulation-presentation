Morse Code (CW)

  • Morse code is one of the earliest digital modes
  • Known to hams as CW ("Continuous Wave")
  • Not actually a continuous wave since it is constantly being interrupted
  • Conceptually an oscillator that is turned on and off by a manually-operated switch (key)

CW Shaping

  • For hams, how nicely you have shaped your keying envelope contributes to your tone
  • Keying hardware has traditionally used RC filters because it's simple to implement
  • RC filters have exponential envelopes instead of gaussian (sinusoidal) so they have sharper edges (but much better than square wave envelopes)

gaussian minimum shift keying

If the rise and fall edges of a square wave are shaped to the same sinusoidal keying waveform, this is referred to as gaussian minimum shift keying — the same thing as amplitude modulating a sine wave signal

CW Bandwidth

  • An unmodulated carrier wave consumes no bandwidth at all (!)
  • Therefore, the bandwidth consumed by a CW transmission depends solely on the keying envelope used
  • Recall from the Amplitude Modulation slides that sinusoidal envelopes consist of two "sideband" signals of slightly different frequencies
  • The bandwidth consumed is the frequency difference between the sidebands of the keying envelope

Phase-shift keying

Constellation diagrams

  • The phase properties of a signal are commonly shown on a complex plane known as a "constellation diagram"
  • All points on the circle represent signals of the same power output so any purely phase-modulated signal must only use symbols on this circle

Binary PSK

  • Binary phase-shift keying uses only two signals that are 180 degrees out-of-phase with each-other

Differential Binary PSK

  • Differential binary phase-shift keying is where the actual phase of the original signal is irrelevant
  • The fast that a phase changes or does not change is how a bit is reconstructed

PSK-31

  • Several variations available but by far most commonly used is known as PSK-31 (technically DBPSK-31.25)
  • Differential Binary Phase-Shift Keying at 31.25 baud
  • Roughly as fast as most people type
  • 31.25 is convenient when using a sample rate of 8000 because the key shaping waveform has a nice even period of 256 samples (32 milliseconds)

Varicode

  • PSK-31 uses a custom encoding called "varicode"
  • Like morse code, frequently used symbols have shorter encodings than rare symbols
  • Self-synchronizing: if you lose your place in a stream you can re-synchronize at the next character (like UTF-8)
  • Encoded symbols never contain adjacent 0s (the character separator)
      " "    : 1
      "e"    : 11
      "t"    : 101
      "b"    : 1011111
      "Q"    : 111011101
      "\xEE" : 101010111101
